\section*{Results}

\subsection*{DESI accurately recovers pairwise coalescent depth across demographic models}

We first validated the PCHMM's ability to recover exact TMRCA depths using coalescent simulations in \texttt{msprime}, spanning models with and without population structure. Across three demographic models — a standard Out-of-Africa scenario (Model OoA), a panmictic ancestral population parameterized following the FitCoal severe bottleneck at $\sim$930 ka (Model A; \citealt{Hu2023}), and a two-population structured ancestral model inspired by Cobraa (Model D; \citealt{Cousins2025}) — the PCHMM recovered mean within-group TMRCA depths within 3.5\% of the exact tree-sequence values in all cases (three replicates per model, seeds 42/123/456; Fig.~\ref{fig:model_compare}b; Table~\ref{tab:calibration}). Recovery accuracy was similar under panmictic (Model A) and structured (Model D) demography, indicating no systematic bias toward inferring structure when none is present.

For Model OoA, PCHMM estimated within-AFR TMRCA of 864.0 ka against an exact value of 845.1 ka ($+2.2\%$) and within-EAS of 379.8 ka against 381.3 ka ($-0.4\%$). Crucially, for Model A, the PCHMM predicted an AFR$-$EAS difference of 0.5 ka (exact: 0.2 ka), confirming that the method does not artifactually inflate between-group differences under panmixia. For Model D, the predicted difference was 396.5 ka (exact: 370.1 ka; $+7.1\%$ relative overestimate), a modest bias attributable to slight overestimation of within-AFR TMRCA ($+3.3\%$); this means that if PCHMM bias were corrected, the inferred structured-model difference would be \textit{smaller} than the observed genome-wide value of 370.7 ka — a conservative direction that does not inflate our main claim.

Having established PCHMM calibration across demographic models, we applied DESI to phased whole-genome data from 240 individuals across five continental super-populations from the 1000 Genomes Project.

\subsection*{African populations retain $\sim$1.4$\times$ deeper genealogical ancestry than East Asians genome-wide}

Across all 27,507 valid 100 kb windows per group spanning the 22 autosomes, within-AFR mean pairwise TMRCA was $1{,}229.5 \pm 6.4$ ka (block-jackknife SE), substantially and significantly deeper than within-EAS ($858.8 \pm 5.4$ ka), within-EUR ($920.2 \pm 5.5$ ka), within-SAS ($984.8 \pm 5.6$ ka), and within-AMR ($950.2 \pm 5.6$ ka) (Fig.~\ref{fig:tmrca_matrix}a; Table~\ref{tab:tmrca_means}). The between-group contrasts involving AFR are all comparably deep (AFR$\times$EAS: $1{,}237.7 \pm 6.6$ ka; AFR$\times$EUR: $1{,}235.2 \pm 6.5$ ka), reflecting that AFR haplotypes carry deep genealogical signal into every cross-population comparison. By contrast, all non-African between-group contrasts cluster within a narrow range of $960$–$994$ ka, suggesting a shared recent ancestor for the non-African clade.

The within-AFR minus within-EAS difference is $370.7 \pm 8.4$ ka ($Z = 44$; Fig.~\ref{fig:tmrca_matrix}b). African populations retain approximately 1.4$\times$ deeper mean genealogical depth than East Asians across the genome — a contrast that SFS-based methods, which assume panmixia, cannot detect because they collapse the ancestral genealogy into a single effective population size trajectory \citep{Cousins2025b}. The magnitude of this difference (370 ka) far exceeds what population-size fluctuations alone would produce under a single-population model, and instead points to a distinct deeper ancestral component that is retained in African genealogies but diluted or absent in non-African ones.

This contrast is not an artifact of any single chromosome or genomic region. Across all 22 autosomes, within-AFR exceeded within-EAS without exception ($Z = 73.1$ from a leave-one-chromosome-out jackknife; jackknife SE = 5.1 ka; range 340.9–440.0 ka; Fig.~\ref{fig:tmrca_matrix}b). The inter-chromosomal coefficient of variation of the difference is only 6.4\%, indicating a spatially homogeneous signal distributed across the full genome.

\subsection*{Panmictic models predict a difference indistinguishable from zero; a structured model matches the observation}

In any panmictic ancestral population, haplotypes sampled from different modern continental groups are interchangeable random draws from the same genealogy. Within-group pairwise TMRCA values must therefore be equal regardless of sampling labels — a fact confirmed analytically by our Model A simulations, in which the exact within-AFR and within-EAS TMRCA depths are 467.7 and 467.4 ka, respectively (difference: 0.2 ka; Fig.~\ref{fig:model_compare}a). The observed difference of 370.7 ka is therefore approximately $\mathbf{1{,}850\times}$ larger than the panmictic prediction. This discrepancy holds regardless of bottleneck parameterization: no adjustment of ancestral $\Ne$ or bottleneck timing within a panmictic framework can produce a $\sim$370 ka AFR$-$EAS contrast, because all modern populations share the same ancestral genealogy under panmixia. SFS-based methods that assume panmixia will necessarily misinterpret genealogical depth differences as fluctuations in a single population's history \citep{Cousins2025b}.

In contrast, Model D — an OoA-like model with a slightly reduced ancestral effective population size ($\Ne = 12{,}000$) motivated by the Cobraa hypothesis \citep{Cousins2025} — predicts an exact AFR$-$EAS difference of 370.1 ka (Fig.~\ref{fig:model_compare}a), matching our observation of 370.7 ka within 0.2\%. Both AFR and EAS trace through the same ancestral lineage in this simulation (the effective genealogy after 60 ka is governed by \emph{popA}, $\Ne = 12{,}000$), but the EAS OoA bottleneck reduces within-EAS TMRCA, creating the depth asymmetry. The smaller ancestral Ne relative to Model OoA ($\Ne = 12{,}000$ vs.\ $15{,}000$) reduces the absolute TMRCA for both groups, shifting the predicted difference from 464 ka (OoA) to 370 ka (Model D), aligning with the observation. This result shows that the observed difference is quantitatively consistent with demographic models that incorporate a smaller ancestral Ne (as suggested by the Cobraa/Cousins framework), and is inconsistent with the strictly panmictic Model A.

We also considered an alternative, which we call the calibrated OoA argument: a single-population OoA model in which the bottleneck is tuned to produce the observed within-EAS mean of 859 ka. We calculated that this scenario requires approximately 31.4\% of within-EAS genomic windows to coalesce during the OoA bottleneck epoch ($\approx$55 ka), producing a bimodal distribution with a large component at $\sim$55 ka. This prediction is directly tested in the following section.

\subsection*{Per-window TMRCA distributions are inconsistent with the calibrated OoA hypothesis}

To examine whether the per-window TMRCA distribution is consistent with the calibrated OoA hypothesis, we fitted Gamma mixture models to within-group per-window TMRCA estimates across all 22 autosomes (Fig.~\ref{fig:kmixture}). We note that each per-window estimate is a group-level aggregate over $\sim$1,300–2,800 pairs; the window-level mean therefore reflects an average across many pairs rather than a single-pair coalescence time, and shallow single-pair coalescence events (e.g., at $\sim$55 ka) would be substantially diluted in this average. The window-level distribution thus tests whether the group-mean TMRCA shows a component near the OoA epoch, rather than testing for isolated single-pair shallow coalescence.

For within-EAS (mean 858.8 ka, s.d. 430.1 ka), BIC-optimal decomposition yielded three components with means at 630, 826, and 969 ka ($\Delta$BIC from K=1: $-5{,}875$). All components lie at depths substantially greater than 100 ka, with zero probability mass below this threshold. The minimum EAS component is centred at $\sim$630 ka — more than six-fold deeper than the $\approx$55 ka epoch of the calibrated OoA bottleneck. Under the calibrated OoA scenario, approximately 31\% of within-EAS windows would be expected to show group-mean TMRCA near the bottleneck epoch; the complete absence of any component at $<$100 ka in the observed distribution is therefore inconsistent with this prediction.

For within-AFR (mean 1,229.5 ka, s.d. 489.0 ka), BIC identified three components at approximately 749, 1,254, and 1,257 ka ($\Delta$BIC from K=1: $-11{,}170$). The greater BIC improvement and larger standard deviation in AFR ($+13.8\%$ relative to EAS) indicate broader genealogical heterogeneity across the African genome — consistent with the retention of lineages from multiple ancestral demes with different coalescent depths. Under ancestral structure, African haplotypes inherit from both proto-Africa and proto-OoA ancestral pools, whereas East Asian haplotypes trace primarily through the shallower proto-OoA pool; this predicts exactly the pattern we observe: deeper and more heterogeneous TMRCA in AFR, and a minimum EAS component that sits far above the OoA bottleneck epoch.

\subsection*{The result is robust to background selection, mutation rate, and genomic architecture}

\paragraph{Background selection.}
Background selection (BGS) reduces effective population size near genes. To assess whether differential BGS between AFR and EAS populations could explain the observed depth contrast, we restricted our analysis to the 30.7\% of the autosomal genome located $>$50 kb from any annotated gene (hg38 RefGene, $\pm$50 kb buffer). In these putatively neutral windows, within-AFR TMRCA was $1{,}197.6 \pm 14.9$ ka and within-EAS was $836.6 \pm 11.7$ ka, yielding a difference of $361.1 \pm 19.0$ ka ($Z = 19.0$; Fig.~\ref{fig:robustness}a) — a decrease of only 2.6\% from the genome-wide estimate (370.7 ka), statistically indistinguishable from it. Importantly, the largest per-chromosome AFR$-$EAS difference was observed on chr19 (440.0 ka), the most gene-dense human autosome; under the BGS hypothesis, this chromosome should show the \textit{smallest} AFR$-$EAS contrast, not the largest. These two lines of evidence — the neutral-window magnitude and the chr19 result — jointly exclude BGS as a meaningful contributor to the depth contrast.

\paragraph{Mutation rate.}
The AFR/EAS TMRCA ratio is $1{,}229.5 / 858.8 = 1.432$. Because TMRCA estimates scale as $\hat{T} \propto 1/\muup$, this ratio is analytically invariant to the assumed mutation rate; we confirm this numerically across $\muup \in \{1.0, 1.2, 1.4, 1.6\} \times 10^{-8}$ per bp per generation (Fig.~\ref{fig:robustness}b). The model-comparison conclusion — panmictic prediction of 0.2 ka vs. observed $\sim$371 ka — is therefore independent of any mutation-rate calibration choice.

\paragraph{Chromosome architecture.}
Acrocentric chromosomes (chr13, 14, 15, 21, 22) and metacentric chromosomes show statistically indistinguishable AFR$-$EAS differences (370.9 vs. 373.4 ka; difference: 2.5 ka, $<0.5\times$ jackknife SE). Chromosome length is not a significant predictor of the difference (Spearman $\rho = -0.37$, $p = 0.09$). Taken together, no common chromosomal confound accounts for the observed pattern.

\subsection*{AMR subpopulation stratification provides a positive-control validation}

Admixed American populations offer a direct test of whether DESI depth estimates track genealogical ancestry rather than technical artifacts: populations with predominantly African ancestry should show within-group TMRCA similar to within-AFR, while those with predominantly indigenous American (East Asian-like) ancestry should match within-EAS. We examined three AMR sub-populations with well-characterized ancestry compositions. Within-ACB (African-Caribbean; predominantly African ancestry) was $1{,}252.8 \pm 11.3$ ka, statistically indistinguishable from within-AFR ($1{,}229.5$ ka; $\Delta = 23$ ka, $<2 \times$ SE of the difference). Within-PEL (Peruvian; predominantly indigenous Andean/East Asian-like ancestry) was $886.7 \pm 70.6$ ka, closely matching within-EAS ($858.8$ ka). Within-PUR (Puerto Rican; African$+$European$+$Native American admixture) was intermediate at $1{,}029.0 \pm 52.9$ ka, consistent with its tripartite modern ancestry (Fig.~\ref{fig:robustness}c).

This correspondence between PCHMM depth and known ancestry composition — with modern admixture events occurring within the past few thousand years — confirms that DESI recovers deep genealogical signal rather than capturing recent population structure or sequencing artifacts. Crucially, it validates the interpretation that the AFR$>$EAS depth contrast reflects genuine deep ancestral genealogical differences, not a methodological bias.
